# Georgia HB 1292 Compliance Checklist

## Standalone Reference for Georgia Notaries

**Law:** Georgia House Bill 1292 (signed May 2, 2024; effective January 1, 2025)  
**Amends:** OCGA Title 44 (Property) and Title 45 (Public Officers), Chapter 17 (Notaries Public)  
**Authority:** Georgia Superior Court Clerks' Cooperative Authority (GSCCCA)  
**Prepared by:** HUMMBL Research Institute — for educational use; not legal advice

---

## Part 1: Who Must Comply

| Requirement | Applies To | Deadline |
|-------------|-----------|----------|
| Mandatory state training course | All **new applicants** and **renewing** notaries | Before commission issued or renewed |
| Journal maintenance | Notaries performing acts for **self-filers** | Every notarization starting Jan 1, 2025 |
| Stricter ID verification | All notaries | Jan 1, 2025 |
| E-filing for self-filers | Clerks of Superior Court (self-filers must use it) | Jan 1, 2025 |

**Bottom line:** If you are a commissioned Georgia notary, HB 1292 affects you. If you notarize real estate documents, it affects you directly.

## Part 2: The 6 Required Journal Fields (OCGA § 45-17-8)

For every notarial act requested by a **self-filer**, you must record:

| # | Field | What to Record | Example |
|---|-------|----------------|---------|
| 1 | **Signer's name** | Full legal name as it appears on the ID | "Jane Marie Doe" |
| 2 | **Signer's address and telephone number** | Current address and phone | "123 Peachtree St, Atlanta, GA 30309; (404) 555-0199" |
| 3 | **Date, time, and location of notarization** | When and where the act occurred | "May 11, 2026, 2:30 PM ET; 123 Peachtree St, Atlanta, GA" |
| 4 | **Type of government-issued photo ID presented, including any identifying number** | ID type + number (do NOT copy the full ID) | "GA Driver's License #DOE123456789" |
| 5 | **Signer's signature** | The signer must sign your journal | [signer's handwritten signature] |
| 6 | **Type of document presented for notarization** | Document category | "Warranty Deed" or "Quitclaim Deed" or "Security Deed" |

### Key Rules About the Journal

- [ ] **Format:** Written (paper) or electronic — your choice, but it must be permanent and tamper-evident
- [ ] **Per-act, not per-document:** If one document requires 3 notarial acts (3 signatures), you need 3 journal entries
- [ ] **Self-filer only?** Technically yes — but **NNA strongly recommends journaling EVERY notarization** because you cannot always tell if the requester is a self-filer until you see the document
- [ ] **Retention:** Keep the journal for the duration of your commission + a reasonable period beyond (industry standard: 10 years)
- [ ] **No erasures:** If you make an error, draw a single line through it, initial the correction, and continue. Never use white-out or remove pages

## Part 3: What Is a "Self-Filer"?

### Definition (OCGA § 44-2-39)

A **self-filer** is any person who is a party to one of the following instruments and who is **NOT** in an exempt profession:

**Covered instruments (must journal if notarized for a self-filer):**
- [ ] Deed
- [ ] Mortgage
- [ ] Lien (as provided by law)
- [ ] Map or plat relating to real estate
- [ ] State tax execution
- [ ] Renewal of state tax execution

**Exempt from self-filer definition (journal NOT required for these requesters):**
- [ ] Licensed attorney
- [ ] Licensed real estate professional
- [ ] Licensed insurance agent
- [ ] Government official performing official duties
- [ ] Title insurance agent or title company representative
- [ ] Lender or financial institution representative

### Practical Rule of Thumb

| If the document is... | And the requester is... | Journal required? |
|----------------------|------------------------|-------------------|
| Deed / Mortgage / Lien / Plat / Tax execution | An individual (not an attorney, realtor, lender, etc.) | **YES** |
| Deed / Mortgage / Lien / Plat / Tax execution | An attorney, realtor, title company, lender | **NO** (they are exempt) |
| General power of attorney, affidavit, contract | Anyone | **No** (not a covered instrument) |
| Acknowledgment / jurat on a non-real-estate doc | Anyone | **No** (not a covered instrument) |

**Pro tip:** When in doubt, journal it. A redundant journal entry is harmless. A missing required journal entry is a compliance violation.

## Part 4: ID Verification Requirements (Post-HB 1292)

### Acceptable IDs Under Georgia Law

The signer must present **one** of the following:

- [ ] **Valid Georgia driver's license** or ID card
- [ ] **Valid out-of-state driver's license** or ID card
- [ ] **Valid U.S. passport** (book or card)
- [ ] **Valid U.S. military ID**
- [ ] **Valid foreign passport** (must be stamped by U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services)
- [ ] **Valid permanent resident card** (green card)

### What to Verify Before Notarizing

- [ ] ID is **not expired** (check expiration date)
- [ ] Photo **reasonably resembles** the signer
- [ ] Name on ID **matches** the name in the document
- [ ] ID has not been **altered or tampered with**
- [ ] If you have **personal knowledge** of the signer, you may rely on that instead of documentary ID (but document it in your journal)

### What NOT to Accept

- [ ] Expired ID (even by one day)
- [ ] photocopy or digital image of an ID (unless specifically authorized by GSCCCA)
- [ ] ID that appears forged, altered, or suspicious
- [ ] Social Security card (not a government-issued photo ID)
- [ ] Credit card, employee badge, or membership card (not acceptable under Georgia law)

## Part 5: Pre-Notarization Workflow Checklist

Use this before every notarization session:

### Step 1: Document Review (2 minutes)

- [ ] Read the document title and identify the document type
- [ ] Determine if it is a covered instrument under HB 1292 (deed, mortgage, lien, plat, tax execution)
- [ ] Check for blank fields (especially signature blocks, notary blocks, dates)
- [ ] Verify the document is complete — do not notarize if pages are missing or blank fields remain
- [ ] Confirm the notary certificate (acknowledgment or jurat) is present and correct
- [ ] For real estate instruments: confirm a Georgia-licensed attorney is involved (attorney-state requirement)

### Step 2: Signer Verification (2 minutes)

- [ ] Signer is physically present (Georgia does not allow RON for GA-commissioned notaries)
- [ ] Signer appears alert, aware, and willing (not under duress)
- [ ] Signer presents acceptable ID (see Part 4)
- [ ] Name on ID matches name in document
- [ ] Photo reasonably resembles signer
- [ ] ID is not expired

### Step 3: Journal Preparation (1 minute)

- [ ] Open your journal (paper or electronic)
- [ ] Confirm you have the 6 required fields ready
- [ ] If the requester is a self-filer (or you are unsure), prepare to make a full journal entry

## Part 6: During-Notarization Checklist

### The Notarial Act

- [ ] Watch the signer sign the document (for jurats) OR confirm the signer acknowledges the signature (for acknowledgments)
- [ ] Verify the notary certificate language matches the act performed:
  - **Acknowledgment:** "The foregoing instrument was acknowledged before me..."
  - **Jurat:** "Sworn to and subscribed before me..."
- [ ] Complete the notary certificate with:
  - [ ] County where the act took place
  - [ ] Date of notarization
  - [ ] Your official signature
  - [ ] Your official stamp/seal (required in Georgia)
  - [ ] Your commission number and expiration date (if your seal does not include them)

### The Journal Entry (if required or if journaling all acts)

- [ ] Record **signer's name**
- [ ] Record **signer's address and telephone number**
- [ ] Record **date, time, and location** of notarization
- [ ] Record **ID type and identifying number**
- [ ] Obtain **signer's signature** in the journal
- [ ] Record **document type**

---

## Part 7: Post-Notarization Checklist

- [ ] Verify the journal entry is complete and legible
- [ ] If electronic journal: confirm the entry is saved and backed up
- [ ] If paper journal: store in a secure, fire-safe location
- [ ] Collect your fee ($2 per notarial act; disclose before performing the act per OCGA § 45-17-11(d))
- [ ] If mobile/travel: add travel fee (unregulated in Georgia; disclose in advance)
- [ ] Provide the signer a copy of the notarized document if requested

## Part 8: Loan Signing Agent Specifics

Loan signings in Georgia involve unique risks because:

1. **Real estate instruments are covered by HB 1292** — journals are required for self-filers
2. **Georgia is an attorney state** — a licensed GA attorney must oversee the closing; you assist, you do not replace the attorney
3. **Loan packages are 100+ pages** — blank notary blocks, missing signatures, and incomplete fields are common
4. **Dry signings cost money** — if you leave with an incomplete package, you must return, burning time and fuel

### Loan Signing Pre-Flight Checklist

Before leaving for the signing:

- [ ] Print and review the full document package
- [ ] Scan for all notary blocks, signature lines, and initial fields
- [ ] Flag any blank or incomplete sections
- [ ] Confirm the borrower's name matches their ID exactly
- [ ] Confirm a Georgia-licensed closing attorney is assigned
- [ ] Pack your notary stamp, journal, and a blue pen (industry standard)

### At the Signing Table

- [ ] Have the borrower sign and initial in ALL required spots before applying your stamp
- [ ] Do not stamp any certificate that is incomplete or incorrect
- [ ] If a notary certificate is missing, STOP — do not create one yourself. Contact the title company or closing attorney
- [ ] Journal each notarial act separately (each notary certificate = one journal entry)
- [ ] For self-filer borrowers: record all 6 HB 1292 fields per entry

## Part 9: Electronic Filing (E-Filing) Context

HB 1292 also requires that **self-filers** e-file the following instruments with the Clerk of Superior Court:

- Deeds
- Mortgages
- Liens
- Maps or plats
- State tax executions

**What this means for notaries:**
- The notary's journal entry becomes part of the e-filing chain
- If a deed is challenged in court, the notary's journal may be subpoenaed
- Accuracy and completeness are now legally critical — not just good practice

## Part 10: Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

| Pitfall | Why It Happens | Fix |
|---------|---------------|-----|
| **Missing journal entry for a self-filer** | Notary didn't realize the requester was a self-filer | Journal every act; let the AI/assistant determine if it was required later |
| **Incomplete journal field** | Rushed, distracted, or unfamiliar with new requirements | Use a checklist (like this one) for every act |
| **Expired ID accepted** | Notary forgot to check the date | Make ID expiration the FIRST thing you check |
| **Name mismatch (ID vs. document)** | Borrower uses nickname, maiden name, or middle initial inconsistently | Ask the signer: "Is this your full legal name as it appears on your ID?" |
| **Notarizing for a signer who seems coerced** | Family member or caregiver hovering, signer seems confused | Refuse the notarization. You are not obligated to perform any act you feel is improper (OCGA § 45-17-8(b)) |
| **Creating a notary certificate when one is missing** | Pressure to complete the signing | NEVER create your own certificate. Contact the title company or attorney |
| **Accepting a photocopied ID** | Signer forgot their wallet | Refuse. Georgia requires the original government-issued photo ID |
| **Failing to disclose fees upfront** | Forgetting the statutory requirement | State your fee ($2 per act + any travel fee) before performing the act |

## Part 11: Training and Renewal

- [ ] Complete the **GSCCCA-approved training course** before applying for or renewing your commission
- [ ] Keep your training certificate on file
- [ ] Track your commission expiration date (4-year term in Georgia)
- [ ] Submit renewal application at least 30 days before expiration
- [ ] Notify the appointing clerk in writing within 30 days of any name or address change
